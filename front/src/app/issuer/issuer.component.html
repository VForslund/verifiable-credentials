Step 2 — The Issuer

An Issuer is a trusted organisation (university, government, telecom…) that creates a digital credential about you, signs it so it can't be forged, and delivers it to your wallet.

📋 What's inside a Verifiable Credential?

Think of it like a digital version of a physical ID card — but one that's impossible to forge, because it's signed with cryptography.

🏷️
Who issued it — the organisation's digital identity, like a tamper-proof stamp from the university, government, or telecom
👤
Who it's about — the facts about you (name, degree, date of birth, phone number, etc.)
📅
When it's valid — an issue date and an expiry date, just like the dates printed on a passport
🔐
A digital signature — a tiny piece of math covering the whole credential. Change a single character anywhere and the signature breaks instantly, so nobody can edit a credential without being caught.

Everything follows the open W3C Verifiable Credentials standard, so any verifier in the world can check the signature without having to call us.

🔗 Why this matters for Truecaller
  • Telecoms as issuers — Telia, Airtel, or any operator issues a VerifiedPhoneCredential proving phone number. Truecaller could skip the OTP costs
  • No API dependency — credentials are self-contained and cryptographically verifiable. Truecaller doesn't need to maintain partnerships or API integrations with every otp provider worldwide.
  • Truecaller as issuer — Truecaller could itself issue Verified Business Caller credentials to enterprise customers, making their identity portable beyond the Truecaller app.
