Step 3 — The Verifier

A Verifier receives a presentation and checks whether it's genuine — no callback to the issuer, no API keys, no third-party service. Everything is checked locally with math.

📋 What does verification actually check?

Imagine someone hands you an ID card. You'd ask: is it real? Is it theirs? Is it still valid? That's exactly what happens here — but the bouncer is a piece of code, and the hologram is replaced by a signature.

📐
Is it shaped right? — the presentation has to follow the standard format. Anything malformed is rejected immediately.
🔐
Is the issuer's signature genuine? — the verifier re-checks the math on the issuer's signature. If anyone changed even one character of the credential, the signature breaks and the credential is rejected.
👤
Is the presenter the real holder? — the presentation also carries the holder's own signature. Without it, a stolen credential is useless.
📅
Is it still valid? — the verifier checks the issue and expiry dates. Expired credentials are rejected, just like an expired passport.
✅
Does it answer the question? — finally, the verifier checks the actual claim it cares about (e.g. "age ≥ 21").

All of this happens locally. See the spec for the full data model.

🔗 Why this matters for Truecaller
  • No cost — When onboarding Truecaller may check for a presentation to onboard users, skipping OTP costs entirely
  • Better UX — Instead of waiting for an SMS, users can just share a credential they already have in their wallet. Hopefully resulting in more user growth
  • Scalability — Since credentials are self-contained we are not at the mercy of providers being able to scale.
